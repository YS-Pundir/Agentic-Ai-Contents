# Pre-read: Plotting & Storytelling with Data

Picture this: your team hands you a spreadsheet with thousands of rows — sales by month, student marks, or customer visits. Someone asks, *“So what is happening?”* You scroll, you squint, you add numbers in your head. Ten minutes later you still are not sure if things are getting better or worse, or where the odd values are hiding.

That feeling is universal. Whether you work in operations, teaching, retail, or you are simply curious about numbers, **raw tables rarely speak clearly**. The skill you are about to build is making those numbers *visible* and *convincing* so anyone in the room can follow you.

---

## When the numbers refuse to tell their story

**What if** you had to spot a wrong entry, a sudden jump in cost, or a trend across a whole year — but you could only look at endless rows? You *could* do it eventually, but it would be slow, tiring, and easy to miss something important.

In real jobs, people want the pattern *visible* fast — not lost in rows of scrolling. That is why teams rely on a repeatable habit: **look at the data carefully first**, then draw the right picture, then explain it in words people remember.

The live session walks you through that full cycle — from a fresh dataset to charts you can explore with your mouse, to a short story your audience can act on.

---

## A habit you already know — applied to data

Before you cook, you check what is in the kitchen: what you have, what is missing, what looks off. **Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)** is the same idea for data. It means taking a new dataset and, in order, asking: How big is it? What does each column mean? Are there gaps? Do the numbers look sensible? Are a few values strangely high or low?

Think of it like a doctor glancing through a patient file before suggesting next steps — a careful first look so nothing obvious is skipped.

Once that check is done, **drawing** becomes purposeful. **Matplotlib** is like pencil and graph paper: you build line charts for change over time (for example, sales month by month), bar charts when you want to **compare** categories side by side (for example, revenue per product), and **histograms** when you want to see how one numeric column is **spread** — how many people scored in this range versus that range. **Plotly** adds another layer: charts that behave like a small dashboard — hover to see exact values, zoom into a period, and explore without rewriting the sheet.

Choosing the wrong picture is like using the wrong map for a journey. The session will give you a simple mental checklist: *What type of data is this? What relationship am I showing — trend, comparison, spread, or link between two numbers? Who is watching — a detail-loving analyst or a busy manager who wants one clear message?*

Finally, **data storytelling** ties it together. A chart alone says *here are some bars*. A story says *sales climbed 40% after we changed strategy — here is the proof, and here is what we should try next.* You will see how titles, fewer distractions, and short labels on the important point can turn a graph into something people remember and repeat.

## Words worth a quick clarity pass

A **distribution** is just the shape of how values spread — bunched in the middle, stretched to one side, or dotted with strange highs and lows. An **outlier** is a value that sits far away from the rest — sometimes a genuine extreme, sometimes a typo (like a salary row with one extra zero). **Bins**, in a histogram, are the ranges the computer groups your numbers into so you can see counts at a glance. None of these ideas need scary maths; they are about *seeing* and *sanity-checking* before you claim anything big.
